
# Unsupervised Learning

Unsupervised learning = training a model on data that has **no labels** — only features (X), no known correct output (y). The model's job is to find hidden **patterns or structure** in the data on its own.

```
Input (Features only)  →  Model  →  Patterns / Structure
```

No "answer key" exists, so there's nothing to check the model's output against — success is judged by how useful/meaningful the discovered structure is.

---

## Example Dataset — 5000 Students (no label this time)

| IQ  | CGPA | Study Hours |
|-----|------|-------------|
| 87  | 7.1  | 4           |
| 111 | 8.9  | 6           |
| 75  | 6.3  | 2           |

Notice there's no `Placement (Y/N)` or `Package` column — we don't know the "right answer" for any row. We just want the model to find structure in IQ/CGPA/Study Hours on its own.

## 1. Clustering
Groups similar data points together into clusters, without being told what the groups should be.

- **Goal:** find natural groupings in the data
- **Example:** grouping the 5000 students into clusters like "high performers," "average," "at-risk" based on IQ/CGPA/study hours — without ever labeling them that way
- **Real-world use:** customer segmentation, grouping similar users for targeted marketing
- **Common algorithms:** K-Means, DBSCAN, Hierarchical Clustering

### Realistic Example — Customer Segmentation (E-commerce)

An online store has data on 10,000 customers: annual spend, purchase frequency, average order value, browsing time.

| Customer | Annual Spend (₹) | Orders/Year | Avg Order Value |
|----------|-------------------|-------------|-------------------|
| A        | 85,000            | 40          | 2,125             |
| B        | 5,000             | 2           | 2,500             |
| C        | 90,000            | 38          | 2,368             |
| D        | 3,000             | 1           | 3,000             |

Running K-Means on this (without ever telling the model what a "group" is) might reveal:

```
Cluster 1: "High-value loyal customers"  → A, C  (spend a lot, order often)
Cluster 2: "Rare one-time buyers"        → B, D  (low spend, few orders)
```

**Business use:** send Cluster 1 customers VIP loyalty perks and early access to sales; send Cluster 2 customers a "we miss you" discount coupon to bring them back. No one manually labeled these customers — the algorithm discovered the groups from raw purchase behavior.

## 2. Dimensionality Reduction
Reduces the number of features while preserving as much important information as possible.

- **Goal:** compress data — go from many columns to fewer, without losing the underlying structure
- **Example:** if you had 50 features about each student (IQ, CGPA, attendance, extracurriculars, etc.), dimensionality reduction could compress them into 2-3 "summary" features that still capture most of the variation — useful for visualization or speeding up other models
- **Real-world use:** visualizing high-dimensional data in 2D/3D, reducing noise, speeding up training
- **Common algorithms:** PCA (Principal Component Analysis), t-SNE, UMAP

### Realistic Example — Compressing Survey Data

A company runs a 50-question employee satisfaction survey (rating 1-5 on things like "work-life balance," "manager support," "pay satisfaction," "growth opportunities," etc.) across 2,000 employees.

That's a table with **2,000 rows × 50 columns** — too many dimensions to plot or reason about directly, and many questions are correlated (e.g., someone unhappy with "manager support" is often also unhappy with "communication").

Applying PCA compresses those 50 correlated questions down to just **2 new combined dimensions**, e.g.:

```
Dimension 1 ≈ "Overall Job Satisfaction"   (blends pay, growth, work-life balance)
Dimension 2 ≈ "Management Quality"          (blends manager support, communication, feedback)
```

**Business use:** HR can now plot all 2,000 employees on a simple 2D scatter plot (Job Satisfaction vs. Management Quality) instead of trying to interpret 50 separate columns — instantly spotting clusters like "low satisfaction + poor management" teams that need attention. This is also commonly used before feeding data into other ML models (like clustering) to speed them up and reduce noise.

## 3. Anomaly Detection
Identifies data points that don't fit the normal pattern — outliers.

- **Goal:** flag unusual/rare data points
- **Example:** a student with `IQ = 300` (physically impossible) or a sudden spike in your MongoDB sync logs would stand out as an anomaly compared to normal data
- **Real-world use:** fraud detection, network intrusion detection, detecting faulty sensor readings, spotting bad data in a pipeline
- **Common algorithms:** Isolation Forest, One-Class SVM, Local Outlier Factor (LOF)

### Realistic Example — Credit Card Fraud Detection

A bank monitors credit card transactions in real time:

| Transaction | Amount (₹) | Location      | Time     |
|-------------|------------|----------------|----------|
| 1           | 500        | Bengaluru      | 2:15 PM  |
| 2           | 1,200      | Bengaluru      | 6:40 PM  |
| 3           | 85,000     | Lagos, Nigeria | 3:02 AM  |
| 4           | 650        | Bengaluru      | 9:10 AM  |

The customer normally spends small amounts (₹500-₹1,200) in Bengaluru during the day. Transaction 3 — a huge amount, in a different country, at 3 AM — doesn't fit the learned "normal" pattern at all.

**Business use:** the model flags Transaction 3 as an anomaly in real time, and the bank can automatically block the card or send an OTP verification before the transaction completes — without ever having seen a labeled example of "this exact fraud pattern" before. This same idea applies directly to your MongoDB → PostgreSQL pipeline: anomaly detection could flag a sudden spike/drop in sync row counts or unusual CDC event patterns as a sign something's broken upstream.

## 4. Association Rule Learning
Finds relationships/rules between variables in large datasets — "if this, then that" patterns.

- **Goal:** discover items or events that frequently occur together
- **Example:** "students who study 6+ hours AND have CGPA > 8.5 also tend to attend extra workshops"
- **Real-world use:** market basket analysis — "customers who buy bread and butter also tend to buy milk," recommendation engines
- **Common algorithms:** Apriori, FP-Growth

### Realistic Example — Shopping Cart (Market Basket Analysis)

Imagine a supermarket has these transactions:

| Transaction | Items Bought                  |
|-------------|--------------------------------|
| 1           | Bread, Butter, Milk            |
| 2           | Bread, Butter                  |
| 3           | Milk, Diapers, Beer            |
| 4           | Bread, Milk, Diapers, Beer     |
| 5           | Bread, Butter, Milk, Diapers   |

Running association rule learning (e.g., Apriori) on this data finds rules like:

```
{Bread, Butter} → {Milk}     (confidence: 80%)
{Diapers} → {Beer}           (confidence: 75%)
```

**How to read this:**
- **Support** — how frequently the itemset appears across all transactions (e.g., `{Bread, Butter}` appears in 3 out of 5 transactions → support = 60%)
- **Confidence** — given someone bought the left side, how often they also bought the right side (e.g., of everyone who bought Bread+Butter, 80% also bought Milk)
- **Lift** — how much more likely the right side is bought *because* of the left side, compared to random chance

**Business use:** the famous (real) "diapers and beer" case — stores discovered these were often bought together and placed them near each other to boost sales. Other everyday applications: "customers who bought this also bought…" on Amazon, combo meal suggestions at fast food chains, or product placement/shelf layout in supermarkets.

## Supervised vs. Unsupervised — Quick Contrast

| | Supervised | Unsupervised |
|---|---|---|
| **Data** | Labeled (X + y) | Unlabeled (X only) |
| **Goal** | Predict a known output | Discover hidden structure |
| **Example** | Predict placement (Y/N) | Group students into clusters |
| **Check correctness?** | Yes, compare to true label | No ground truth to compare to |
